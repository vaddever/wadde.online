The Conditions

Two conditions, often confused — and often together.

Endometriosis and adenomyosis are chronic conditions where tissue similar to the lining of the uterus grows where it shouldn't. They share many symptoms, can occur at the same time, and are both frequently dismissed or diagnosed late. Here's what they actually are.

What it is

Endometriosis is a chronic, inflammatory condition in which tissue similar to the endometrium — the lining of the uterus — grows outside the uterus. These growths, called lesions, are most often found in the pelvis: on the ovaries, fallopian tubes, the tissue lining the pelvis, and sometimes the bowel or bladder. In rarer cases lesions appear elsewhere in the body.

Each cycle this tissue responds to hormones much like the uterine lining does — it thickens and bleeds — but it has no way to leave the body. The result is inflammation, scar tissue (adhesions), and often significant pain.

Common symptoms

  • Severe, often debilitating period pain (dysmenorrhea)
  • Chronic pelvic pain that continues between periods
  • Pain during or after sex (dyspareunia)
  • Painful bowel movements or urination, especially during periods
  • Heavy or irregular bleeding
  • Bloating and nausea ("endo belly"), fatigue
  • Difficulty getting pregnant (affects an estimated 30–50%)

Symptoms vary enormously from person to person, and severity doesn't always match the amount of disease present — which is part of why it is so often missed.

How it's diagnosed

Diagnosis has historically required laparoscopy — keyhole surgery to view and biopsy lesions, still considered the gold standard for definitive diagnosis. Today, international guidelines also support a "clinical diagnosis" based on symptoms plus imaging such as transvaginal ultrasound or MRI, so that treatment can begin without waiting for surgery. Newer symptom checklists and lab tests are emerging.

How it's managed

There is currently no cure, but symptoms can often be managed well. Approaches include anti-inflammatory pain relief (NSAIDs), hormonal therapies (combined or progestin-only contraceptives, hormonal IUDs), GnRH agonists or the newer oral GnRH antagonists, and surgery to remove lesions. Increasingly, care also includes nutrition, physiotherapy, pain specialists and mental-health support — treating the whole person, not just the pelvis.

What it is

Adenomyosis is a condition in which tissue similar to the uterine lining grows into the muscular wall of the uterus (the myometrium) — rather than outside the uterus as in endometriosis. The trapped tissue still responds to the menstrual cycle, causing the uterine wall to thicken, the uterus to enlarge and become tender, and periods to become heavy and painful.

It can be focal (confined to one area) or diffuse (spread throughout the uterine wall). It's possible to have both adenomyosis and endometriosis at the same time.

Common symptoms

  • Heavy, prolonged menstrual bleeding
  • Severe cramping and period pain
  • Chronic pelvic pain or a feeling of pressure/heaviness
  • An enlarged, tender uterus
  • Bloating and abdominal discomfort
  • Pain during sex

A useful (if imperfect) distinction: adenomyosis pain is often more constant and centred on the uterus, while classic endometriosis pain tends to flare cyclically and can radiate more widely. But the overlap is large.

How it's diagnosed

Adenomyosis is usually identified through imaging — transvaginal ultrasound or MRI — which can show thickening and changes in the uterine wall. Because symptoms overlap with fibroids and other conditions, a careful clinical assessment matters. Historically a definitive diagnosis was only confirmed after hysterectomy, but imaging now allows diagnosis without surgery in most cases.

How it's managed

Treatment focuses on controlling pain and heavy bleeding: NSAIDs, hormonal options (the pill, hormonal IUD, progestins), and non-hormonal medicines such as tranexamic acid to reduce bleeding. Uterine artery / fibroid embolisation can help some people. Hysterectomy — removing the uterus — is the one definitive cure, but it ends fertility, so it's reserved for those who have completed their families or have severe, treatment-resistant symptoms.

Find a Specialist

Getting the right care matters.

Endometriosis and adenomyosis are best managed by clinicians who specialise in them. A general practitioner is a good first step, but for diagnosis and surgery you want someone with specific expertise.

How to find the right specialist

  • Start with a gynaecologist — ideally one with a stated interest in endometriosis/adenomyosis or pelvic pain.
  • Look for an accredited endometriosis centre or a surgeon experienced in excision surgery if surgery is on the table.
  • Ask about a multidisciplinary team — the best care often combines gynaecology, pain management, physiotherapy, and mental-health support.
  • Keep a symptom diary before your appointment (the Self-Check below can generate one) so nothing gets forgotten.
  • Questions worth asking: How many cases like mine do you see? What are my diagnosis and treatment options? What happens if the first approach doesn't work?

Support & Community

You don't have to carry this alone.

Chronic pain affects far more than the body. Whatever stage you're at — newly suspecting something, freshly diagnosed, or years into managing it — there is support and there are strategies that help.

🫂 Peer & emotional support

Connecting with others who understand reduces isolation and helps you feel believed.

  • Join a local or online support group for endometriosis/adenomyosis.
  • Bring a trusted friend or family member to appointments.
  • Consider talking to a counsellor or psychologist — chronic pain and anxiety/low mood often travel together, and support helps.

🛠️ Practical coping strategies

Small, consistent tools can make daily life more manageable alongside medical treatment.

  • Keep a symptom and pain diary to spot patterns and inform your care.
  • Heat, gentle movement, rest and pacing can ease flares for many.
  • Ask your care team about pelvic-floor physiotherapy and pain-management services.

🗣️ Advocating for yourself

Many people are dismissed for years. Being prepared helps you be heard.

  • Write down your symptoms, how long, and how they affect your life.
  • Be specific about pain severity and its impact on work, sleep and relationships.
  • It's okay to ask for a referral to a specialist or a second opinion.

💛 If things feel overwhelming

Living with persistent pain can take a real toll on mental health. That is understandable, and help is available.

  • Reach out to your doctor about how you're coping emotionally, not just physically.
  • If you ever feel unable to cope or unsafe, please contact a trusted person or a local health professional or helpline right away.
  • You deserve care for your whole self.
